
# Books for Competitive Programming & DSA

A curated reading list, organized by purpose. Books marked with an asterisk (*) are **personal favourites of the maintainer** — see my journey for the full story behind why.

---

## How to Use This List

You do not need to read all of these. Honestly, you don't even need to read most of them. Pick **one beginner book + one algorithms book + one reference**, and you have everything.

Read books *alongside practice* 
1. Read a chapter or section.
2. Implement the algorithm yourself, from scratch, without copying.
3. Solve 5–10 LeetCode or Codeforces problems on that topic.
4. Move on. Come back later when you forget.

If a chapter feels too dense, skip it and come back later. Books are tools, not exams.

## Start Here — Beginner-Friendly

### Competitive Programmer's Handbook — Antti Laaksonen **[Free]**
The single best free CP book. 290 pages, written specifically for the contest crowd. Compact, no fluff.
- **Free PDF:** [cses.fi/book/book.pdf](https://cses.fi/book/book.pdf)
- **Best for:** Anyone who has done a few HackerRank or LeetCode problems and wants structure
- **Pair with:** The CSES Problem Set (same author) — practice as you read
- **My note:** The **Segment Tree** and **Binary Indexed Tree** sections in this book are especially easy to understand. Worth reading those chapters even if you skim the rest.

## The Maintainer's Three — Read These

These are the books I personally used and keep recommending. Listed in the order I'd read them if I were starting today.

### 1. Algorithms — Jeff Erickson **[Free]** *
I studied this recently and it is genuinely the best-structured algorithms book I have come across. Clear explanations, good problems at the end of every chapter, free, and the author has put real thought into how concepts build on each other. **If you read only one algorithms book, make it this one.**
- **Free PDF:** [jeffe.cs.illinois.edu/teaching/algorithms](https://jeffe.cs.illinois.edu/teaching/algorithms/)
- **Best for:** Building a strong algorithmic foundation from scratch
- **Standout chapters:** Recursion, Backtracking, Dynamic Programming, Graph Algorithms
- **My note:** Take your time with the recursion and DP chapters. They're the ones that pay off forever.

### 2. Guide to Competitive Programming — Antti Laaksonen *
The expanded paid version of the free CP Handbook (same author). I keep coming back to specific sections of this book whenever I am rusty:
- The **second method for Binary Search** (the "highest power of 2 jump" approach)
- **Section 5.3** — complete search techniques.
- The **Binary Indexed Tree (Fenwick)** chapter.
- The **Segment Tree** chapter.

I read this years ago and these are the parts I still re-open.
- **Best for:** A reference you actually re-read, not just shelve
- **Format:** Available in print (Springer) and digital

### 3. Competitive Programming 3 / 4 — Steven & Felix Halim *
The classic ICPC handbook. I haven't read the whole thing — but the **initial chapters are gold for understanding what the CP world even is**: the contest culture, the types of problems, the meta-strategy of competitive programming itself. Many thanks to the authors for that introduction. Use the rest of the book as a reference when you encounter a topic you don't know.
- **CP4 is the latest edition.** CP3 is also fine if that's what you have.
- **Best for:** Understanding the CP ecosystem and ICPC-style problem solving
- **My honest take:** Read chapters 1 and 2 carefully. Skim the rest. Come back when needed.

### Bonus: How to Solve It — George Pólya ( My First Book about Computer science) 
A classic from 1945. Not an algorithms book — it is about **how to think** about a problem before you write any code. Short, simple, timeless. The four-step method (Understand → Plan → Execute → Review) is the most useful framework I know when I am stuck.
- **My note:** Most "I can't solve this" moments are actually "I haven't understood the problem yet" moments. This book fixes that.

## The Bigger Reference Books

These are the heavyweight textbooks. To be honest, I have not read most of them cover to cover — and I don't think you need to either. But each one is worth knowing about, because at some point you will hit a topic where you want a deeper or more rigorous treatment, and these are where to look.

### Introduction to Algorithms (CLRS) — Cormen, Leiserson, Rivest, Stein
Honestly, I have not read this front to back, and I don't think most people should. It is over 1300 pages and reads more like a math textbook than a programming book. But every time I forget *why* a specific algorithm works, this is what I reach for. Treat it as a lookup book, not a read-through. ( Cheap on Ebay )
- **Best used as:** A reference for proofs and formal definitions
- **Skip if:** You want to start solving problems quickly — Erickson is enough for that


### The Algorithm Design Manual — Steven Skiena
I haven't read this one in full. It has a strong reputation in the CP community though — more practical than CLRS, with real-world stories from the author's consulting work mixed in. The catalog of algorithms at the back is the part most people end up using.


## The Practical Recommendation


1. **Striver's playlist**  — videos move faster than books to start with.
2. **Competitive Programmer's Handbook** (free, ~1 week skim) — to see what CP looks like; the BIT and Segment Tree chapters are especially clean.
3. **Algorithms by Jeff Erickson** (2–3 months, alongside practice) — this is your real foundation.
4. **Guide to Competitive Programming** — referenced for specific topics (binary search, BIT, segment tree).
5. **CP3 / CP4 initial chapters** (one weekend) — to understand the CP world itself.

That's it.
