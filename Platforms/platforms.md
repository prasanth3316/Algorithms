
# Coding Platforms — Where to Practice and Compete

A guide to the platforms I have used (and a few I haven't but should be on your radar), organized by purpose. 

For my full journey through these platforms, see my Journey.

---

## How to Use This List

You do not need an account on every platform here. Honestly, two or three is enough for most people. The trap is hopping between platforms instead of *getting better* on any one of them.

My rough rule:
- **One platform for daily practice** (LeetCode for me).
- **One platform for contests** (LeetCode contests early, then Codeforces later).
- **One platform for reading** (GeeksforGeeks, when I want a written explanation).

Everything else is optional.

---

## The Platforms I Actually Used (In Order)

These are the four platforms my own journey ran through, in the order I touched them.

### 1. HackerRank — [hackerrank.com](https://www.hackerrank.com/)
Where I started. Honestly, the friendliest platform out there for someone who is still learning a language. The domain structure (Algorithms, Data Structures, Strings, Math, etc.) gives you a clear path, and the star and badge system kept me hooked early on. The test-case pass/fail loop is genuinely fun when you are new to it.
- **Best for:** Anyone who is still learning their first language and wants instant feedback
- **My take:** This is the right place to be while you are still figuring out syntax. Once you can solve most of the easy and medium-easy problems comfortably, move on.
- **Skip if:** You are already comfortable with arrays, strings, and basic loops in your language

### 2. LeetCode — [leetcode.com](https://leetcode.com/)
My main practice ground. I have spent more time here than anywhere else. The reasons are simple: huge problem set, clean UI, every problem has a discussion section with multiple solutions, the daily question keeps you consistent, and **a lot of the questions are literally what gets asked in interviews**.
- **Best for:** Topic-wise practice while you learn, and serious interview preparation
- **My take:** If you do nothing else but the LC daily for a year, you will be stronger than most people who claim to "study CP". Quantity with reflection turns into quality.
- **Pro tip:** Use the **tag filter** to grind a specific topic when you are learning it. Solve 10–15 problems on the same topic in a row and you will feel the pattern.
- **Premium:** Worth it if you are doing FAANG interview prep (company-tagged problems are useful). Otherwise, the free tier is more than enough.

### 3. CodeChef — [codechef.com](https://www.codechef.com/)
The next step up from LeetCode for me. CodeChef has a strong Indian community, regular contests (Starters every Wednesday), and slightly harder problems than LC.
- **Best for:** Slightly harder problem-solving and contest exposure without Codeforces-level brutality
- **My take:** Good middle ground between LC contests and CF rounds. Land here after LC Medium feels easy.

### 4. Codeforces — [codeforces.com](https://codeforces.com/)
The global standard for competitive programming. This is where the actual CP world lives — the largest contest community, the most contests per week, the most blog content, and the real ratings everyone talks about. But honestly, the speed and difficulty of CF rounds can be brutal early on, and the rating drops will sting.
- **Best for:** Real competitive programming, once you are ready


### 5. GeeksforGeeks — [geeksforgeeks.org](https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/)
Not strictly a contest platform, but I am putting it here because **I use it almost daily**. It is my go-to when I want to *read* an explanation rather than watch a video. The article + code + practice combo on most topics is excellent for self-study. The Problem of the Day is also a nice consistency habit.
- **Best for:** Reading clear written explanations of standard problems and topics
- **My take:** Pair it with LC. **LC for practice, GfG for understanding.** That combination got me through most of my interview prep.

## Other Major Platforms (Worth Knowing)

I have not used these as much as the four above, but each one has a real reason to exist. Listed honestly with what I do and don't know about them.

### AtCoder — [atcoder.jp](https://atcoder.jp/)
I have not used AtCoder as much as I should have. The problems have a strong reputation in the CP community for being clean, well-tested, and more math-flavoured than CF. The **AtCoder Beginner Contest (ABC)** every Saturday is widely regarded as one of the best beginner-friendly contests in the world.
- **Best for:** Cleaner, more "thinking" problems; great Saturday habit
- **Honest note:** Many top competitive programmers say AtCoder is their favourite judge over Codeforces. If I were starting today, I would probably make ABC a fixed weekly habit.

### CSES Problem Set — [cses.fi/problemset](https://cses.fi/problemset/)
Same author as the Competitive Programmer's Handbook (Antti Laaksonen). The problem set is **topic-wise** and very well structured — Introductory, Sorting & Searching, Dynamic Programming, Graph Algorithms, Range Queries, Tree Algorithms, Mathematics, String Algorithms, Geometry, and Advanced Techniques.
- **Best for:** Learning a topic by solving problems on it back-to-back
- **My take:** This is the closest thing to a structured curriculum on an online judge. If you are reading a CP book and want matching problems, this is the place.

### USACO — [usaco.org](https://usaco.org/)
Mainly for high schoolers. The contest structure is **Bronze → Silver → Gold → Platinum**, and you compete against other students at your division. The companion **USACO Guide** ([usaco.guide](https://usaco.guide/)) is one of the best free CP curriculums I have come across.
- **Best for:** High school students in any country
- **My take:** Even if you are out of high school, USACO Guide is worth using as a reading resource.

### Project Euler — [projecteuler.net](https://projecteuler.net/)
Math-heavy problems. Not "CP" in the contest sense — there are no time limits or rankings — but the problems are genuinely beautiful and force you to think mathematically.
- **Best for:** When you want a different flavour, or to sharpen number-theory intuition

### Kattis — [open.kattis.com](https://open.kattis.com/)
ICPC-style judge used by many regional ICPC contests. Clean problem statements, supports many languages, and lots of classic problems.
- **Best for:** ICPC preparation

### SPOJ — [spoj.com](https://www.spoj.com/)
The old-school judge. Lots of niche classic problems, but the platform feels dated and submissions can be flaky. You will see SPOJ links a lot in older tutorials and blogs.
- **Best for:** Specific classic problems referenced in older CP material

### DMOJ — [dmoj.ca](https://dmoj.ca/)
Canadian online judge. Fast and clean, hosts problems from many regional and national contests.
- **Best for:** Regional contest archives

### Library Checker — [judge.yosupo.jp](https://judge.yosupo.jp/)
Not for general practice — this one is for **verifying your own implementations** of advanced data structures (segment trees, suffix arrays, FFT, etc.) against known-correct test cases.
- **Best for:** Advanced users stress-testing their template code
- **My take:** I haven't used it personally, but if you are writing your own advanced templates, this is the right place to confirm they work.

### TopCoder — [topcoder.com](https://www.topcoder.com/)
A historical giant in CP — the SRMs (Single Round Matches) used to be the place to compete. Less active today, but the problem archives and tutorials are still gold.
- **Best for:** Reading classic algorithm tutorials

### HackerEarth — [hackerearth.com](https://www.hackerearth.com/)
Indian platform with regular contests, especially hiring-style competitions sponsored by companies. Decent practice problems too.
- **Best for:** Company-sponsored hiring contests

## Hiring & Flagship Competitions

These are not regular practice platforms — they are events you participate in once or twice a year. I have tried a few of these myself.

### TCS CodeVita
An India-flagship hiring contest from TCS. Multiple rounds, hard problems, opens roughly once a year. I tried this and **never solved more than 4 problems** — they are genuinely difficult. But the experience is great, and it looks good on a resume.
- **Best for:** Indian engineering students
- **My take:** Attempt it for the experience, not for the win

### Infosys HackWithInfy
Similar to CodeVita but from Infosys. Open to engineering students in India. Hard problems, three rounds, real recruitment opportunities at the end.
- **Best for:** Indian engineering students seeking placement opportunities

### Meta Hacker Cup — [facebook.com/codingcompetitions/hacker-cup](https://www.facebook.com/codingcompetitions/hacker-cup/)
Meta's annual global CP contest. Open to everyone, real prize money in the finals. I have not participated, but it is well-respected in the CP community.
- **Best for:** Anyone who wants a serious, globally-ranked annual contest

### ICPC — [icpc.global](https://icpc.global/)
The flagship academic CP contest. Team of 3, regional rounds → world finals. If you are at university, **joining an ICPC team is one of the most valuable CP experiences possible** — you learn to work with others, manage time under pressure, and read problems fast.
- **Best for:** University students
- **My take:** If your university has an ICPC club, join it. If it doesn't, start one.

### Google Code Jam / Kick Start
**Discontinued** as of 2023, but the problem archives are still online and are some of the cleanest CP problems ever written. Worth using as a problem source even though the contests no longer run.

## The Practical Recommendation

If you are starting today and asked me "where should I be solving":

| Stage | Where | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Just learning a language | **HackerRank** | Friendly, structured, instant feedback |
| Comfortable with basics | **LeetCode** (daily problem + topic grind) | Largest interview-relevant problem set |
| Stuck on a topic | **GeeksforGeeks** (read) + **LC tag filter** (practice) | Read → understand → solve |
| Want structured topic practice | **CSES Problem Set** | Best curriculum on any judge |
| Ready for contests | **LeetCode Weekly** → **CodeChef Starters** → **Codeforces Div. 3/4** | Gradually increase difficulty |
| Ready for the real thing | **Codeforces Div. 2** | The real CP world |
| Once or twice a year | **TCS CodeVita / HackWithInfy / Meta Hacker Cup** | Big-event experience |
| If your situation fits | **USACO** (high school) / **ICPC** (university) | Community + structure |

## A Few Honest Notes

- **You do not need accounts everywhere.** Pick two or three platforms and actually use them. Hopping between platforms is procrastination dressed up as preparation.
- **Ratings are useful as feedback, not as identity.** Your CF rating going from 1100 to 1300 means you got better. It doesn't mean you are 1300 worth of human.
- **The "right" platform is the one you actually open every day.** I would rather have someone do the LC daily for a year on autopilot than have someone draft a perfect cross-platform plan and quit in two weeks.
- **Stuck on a problem for over an hour?** Read the editorial, then watch a YouTube video on it if needed, then re-implement it from scratch a day later. Stuck doesn't mean "spend three hours staring".
